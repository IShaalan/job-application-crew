# Designer Role Pack -- Seed content, improves as you use it

This role pack provides positioning strategies, keyword priorities, and structural guidance for design applications. As you use the system, battle-tested learnings from real applications will be added here automatically.

---

## How to Use This File

When preparing a new application:
1. Identify the role subtype from the categories below
2. Review positioning angles and keywords for that subtype
3. Adapt the approach for the specific company/role
4. Add new learnings after application completion

## Role Subtype: Product Designer

### What They're Looking For
- End-to-end design ownership (research through high-fidelity UI)
- Strong systems thinking -- understanding how features connect across the product
- Data-informed design decisions (not just aesthetics)
- Cross-functional collaboration with engineering and product management
- Ability to balance user needs with business goals

### Positioning Angles
1. **"Outcome-driven designer"**: Lead with business metrics your designs moved (conversion, retention, task completion), not just the visual quality of your work
2. **"Full-stack designer"**: Show you can do research, wireframes, visual design, and prototyping -- not just one slice. Startups especially value range.
3. **"Systems builder"**: Demonstrate you think in patterns and components, not just screens. Show design systems, reusable patterns, and scalable solutions.

### Keywords
- Product design, UX/UI design, interaction design
- Figma, Sketch, Adobe XD, Framer
- Design systems, component libraries
- User research, usability testing
- Prototyping, wireframing, user flows
- Responsive design, mobile-first
- Accessibility, WCAG compliance
- Data-informed design, A/B testing
- Cross-functional collaboration
- Information architecture

### Portfolio Integration Guidance
- **Case study structure**: Problem -> Process -> Solution -> Impact. Every case study needs measurable impact.
- **Show process, not just output**: Include research artifacts, early sketches, iterations, and failed approaches. HMs want to see how you think.
- **3-5 case studies max**: Quality over quantity. Each should demonstrate a different skill or domain.
- **Tailor the order**: Put the most relevant case study first for each application. If the role is B2B SaaS, lead with your B2B SaaS case study.
- **Include constraints**: Budget, timeline, technical limitations -- showing you design within constraints is more impressive than unconstrained design.

## Role Subtype: UX Researcher

### What They're Looking For
- Mixed-methods expertise (qualitative + quantitative)
- Ability to synthesize research into actionable product recommendations
- Strong storytelling and stakeholder communication
- Research operations and scaling research practice
- Impact measurement -- how research influenced product decisions

### Positioning Angles
1. **"Research that ships"**: Show your research led to specific product decisions, not just reports that sat on a shelf. Name the feature, the change, the metric.
2. **"Mixed-methods strategist"**: Demonstrate range across interviews, surveys, usability studies, analytics, and diary studies -- and show judgment about when to use each.
3. **"Research democratizer"**: Show you've scaled research beyond just yourself by building templates, training PMs/designers to do lightweight research, or building insight repositories.

### Keywords
- User research, UX research, qualitative research, quantitative research
- Usability testing, user interviews, contextual inquiry
- Surveys, diary studies, card sorting, tree testing
- Research synthesis, affinity mapping, journey mapping
- Research operations, insight repositories
- A/B testing, analytics, behavioral data
- Personas, jobs-to-be-done, mental models
- Stakeholder communication, research presentations
- Mixed methods, triangulation
- Research ethics, consent, privacy

### Portfolio Integration Guidance
- **Research case studies**: Focus on the insight and its impact, not just the methodology. "We discovered X, which led to Y change, resulting in Z metric improvement."
- **Show your recommendation framework**: How do you go from raw data to actionable recommendations? HMs want to see analytical rigor.
- **Include failed hypotheses**: Showing that research disproved an assumption is just as valuable as confirming one -- it demonstrates intellectual honesty.
- **Anonymize responsibly**: Show enough detail to demonstrate rigor without violating participant privacy or NDA.

## Role Subtype: Visual / Brand Designer

### What They're Looking For
- Strong visual craft (typography, color, composition, layout)
- Brand strategy and identity design experience
- Ability to create and maintain brand guidelines at scale
- Motion design and illustration skills (increasingly expected)
- Understanding of how brand translates across digital and physical touchpoints

### Positioning Angles
1. **"Brand systems architect"**: Show you build brand systems that scale, not just one-off designs. Guidelines, templates, and asset libraries demonstrate maturity.
2. **"Craft + strategy"**: Lead with the strategic rationale behind visual decisions, not just the aesthetics. Why this color? Why this typeface? Connect visual choices to brand positioning.
3. **"Cross-platform consistency"**: Demonstrate experience ensuring brand coherence across web, mobile, email, social, print, and any other touchpoint.

### Keywords
- Visual design, brand design, graphic design
- Brand identity, brand guidelines, brand systems
- Typography, color theory, composition, layout
- Figma, Illustrator, Photoshop, After Effects
- Motion design, animation, micro-interactions
- Illustration, iconography, photography direction
- Design tokens, theming
- Marketing design, campaign creative
- Print design, packaging (if relevant)
- Presentation design, pitch decks

### Portfolio Integration Guidance
- **Show range**: Include brand identity work, marketing creative, product UI, and motion design to demonstrate versatility.
- **Before/after**: Brand refreshes are powerful when shown as before/after with rationale for the change.
- **Show the system, not just the logo**: A logo is table stakes. Show the guidelines, the component library, the application across touchpoints.
- **Include motion**: Even a few animated examples signal you understand modern brand expression.
- **Credit collaborative work**: Be clear about your role vs. the team's contribution. HMs respect honesty about scope.

## Role Subtype: Design Lead / Manager

### What They're Looking For
- Track record of building and growing design teams
- Design process and operations expertise
- Ability to influence product strategy at the leadership level
- Mentoring and career development for designers
- Balance between hands-on craft and strategic leadership

### Positioning Angles
1. **"Player-coach"**: Show you can still do hands-on design work while leading a team. Most companies hiring their first design lead want both.
2. **"Design culture builder"**: Demonstrate you've established critique practices, design principles, and career frameworks that elevated the entire team's output.
3. **"Strategic design partner"**: Show you sit at the product strategy table, not just the execution table. Examples of influencing roadmap, business decisions, or org structure through design thinking.

### Keywords
- Design leadership, design management, people management
- Design operations, design systems
- Team building, hiring, mentoring, career development
- Design critique, design principles, design culture
- Product strategy, roadmap influence
- Stakeholder management, executive communication
- Cross-functional leadership
- Design thinking, workshop facilitation
- OKRs, design metrics, design quality
- Organizational design, team structure

### Portfolio Integration Guidance
- **Show team outcomes, not just personal work**: Include examples where your team produced great work under your leadership. Credit the team.
- **Include process artifacts**: How you run design reviews, your critique framework, your career ladder -- these show management maturity.
- **Case study: "Team I built"**: A case study about growing a team from 2 to 8, or improving design quality across the org, is as valuable as a product case study.
- **Show your management philosophy**: HMs want to know how you think about design leadership, not just what you've done.
- **Include speaking/writing**: Blog posts, conference talks, or workshop facilitation demonstrate thought leadership.

## Cross-Cutting Guidance

### Resume Structure for Designers
- **Contact line**: Include portfolio URL prominently. It's the most important link on a designer's resume.
- **Summary**: 2-3 sentences. Years of experience + design specialization + scale indicators (users impacted, team size, products shipped). Lead with outcomes, not tools.
- **Skills**: Group logically (Design Tools | Research Methods | Technical Skills). Order by JD relevance.
- **Experience bullets**: Lead with impact, not activity. "Increased onboarding completion by 34%" beats "Redesigned the onboarding flow."
- **Side projects**: Include if they demonstrate skills not shown in work experience. Personal projects, community contributions, open-source design work.

### Bullet Writing Rules
- Start with a strong action verb (Designed, Led, Established, Built, Conducted, Shipped, Improved)
- Include a metric in every bullet (completion rate, satisfaction score, support ticket reduction, time-on-task)
- Keep bullets under 25 words
- End every bullet with a period
- Avoid: "Responsible for", "Helped with", "Worked on", "Assisted in"

### Common Mistakes
- **Portfolio link missing or broken**: This is the #1 rejection reason for designers. Test your link before every application.
- **Listing tools without impact**: "Proficient in Figma" tells the HM nothing. Show what you built with Figma and what it achieved.
- **No metrics**: Design is increasingly expected to show measurable impact. If you don't have exact numbers, use directional indicators ("reduced by ~30%").
- **Process without outcome**: Showing your process is important, but if the case study ends at "we shipped it" without impact data, it falls flat.
- **One-size-fits-all portfolio**: Reorder case studies for each application. The most relevant work should be first.

### Cover Letter Tips for Designers
- Reference a specific design decision in the company's product -- shows you've done your homework
- Connect your design philosophy to the company's design challenges
- Mention your portfolio explicitly and guide the reader to the most relevant case study
- 200-300 words, 3 paragraphs max
- Show personality -- design roles value communication style more than most

### Design Challenge / Take-Home Preparation
- Ask clarifying questions before starting (scope, timeline, success criteria)
- Document your process and trade-offs, not just the final output
- Present 2-3 options with rationale, not just one solution
- Include a "what I'd do with more time" section
- Keep to the stated time limit -- going over signals poor time management
